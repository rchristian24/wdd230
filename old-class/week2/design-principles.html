Fitts Law

Hotjar

www.hotjar.com

The Hotjar homepage is a good example of the principle of Fitts Law. According to Interaction Design Foundation on their website, interaction-design.org, "Fitts’ law states that the amount of time required for a person to move a pointer (e.g., mouse cursor) to a target area is a function of the distance to the target divided by the size of the target. Thus, the longer the distance and the smaller the target’s size, the longer it takes." What this means, in regards to web design is that the bigger the button to click on, the more accessible it is to the user and much more likely to be clicked. Hotjar's website shows this principle by drawing all of the users attention to the buttons on the screen. They do this through size (making the buttons bigger than the type), color(making the color bold and standout different from the rest fo the page), and location in relation to other elements (by placing the buttons where the users eyes are more likely to look - the top and middle/left of the page).

Repetition

Nike

www.nike.com

Nike is a perfect example of repetition. Most people know Nike's logo of the 'swoop' because of the repetitive way they use it. Not only is it everywhere on their merchandise, but small and large images of their logo are scattered throughout their webpage. By placing their well known swoop in areas where the users eyes naturally fall, Nike is cementing their brand further into the users mind.

White Space Design

Dropbox

www.dropbox.com

Dropbox utilizes their white space in a very professional manner. Their website is jampacked with information but it does not feel that way because of the way DropBox is able to incorporate white space into its design. They have many icons that they then give a title, then a brief description as well as having links to other pages. Space surrounds each of these points, creating a flow fleeing instead of being crammed together.
